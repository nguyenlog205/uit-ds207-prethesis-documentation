\section{Conclusion and Future Work}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This paper introduced AvocadoLeaf, a 1,861-image dataset of five field-collected avocado leaf disease categories and healthy leaves, and used it to benchmark 15 lightweight and efficient architectures for edge-deployable disease classification. ConvNeXt‑V2 offered the best accuracy-efficiency trade-off, reaching 92.14\% accuracy and 0.900 macro F1 with only 3.4M parameters and 2.30 ms per-image inference on a single GPU. Metric-based few-shot learning with a frozen ConvNeXt‑V2 backbone more than doubled the accuracy of conventional fine-tuning at every shot level tested (e.g. 57.4\% vs. 28.6\% at 20 shots), and uncertainty estimation showed that the model reliably separates in-distribution avocado leaves from both unrelated images (far OOD AUC 0.994) and leaves of other crop species (near OOD AUC 0.985), while a full-test-set LIME deletion-metric evaluation confirmed that its predictions localise to disease-relevant leaf regions rather than background artefacts (mean confidence drop 0.649 when the attributed region is hidden, 95\% CI [0.618, 0.678], $n=280$). Together, these results indicate that an accurate, compact, and interpretable classifier can be built for avocado disease diagnosis under realistic, field-collected conditions.

At the same time, Section~\ref{sec:discussion} identified concrete gaps that should be closed before deployment: the model's raw confidence is only moderately calibrated (ECE 0.0625, above the common 0.05 threshold), no on-device latency or pruning results yet exist to substantiate edge-deployment claims, and no systematic error analysis has been performed. Future work will therefore prioritise (1) completing the structured-pruning and on-device (e.g. Raspberry Pi, mobile) latency measurements already scaffolded in this project, (2) applying post-hoc calibration such as temperature scaling, and (3) a confusion-matrix-driven error analysis to characterise systematic failure modes, particularly for the visually similar and lower-support classes. Beyond these, we plan to expand the dataset to more growing regions and to investigate why RelationNet failed to learn a useful metric in the few-shot setting, in contrast to ProtoNet. Longer-term directions include pairing the classifier with a large language model to turn its predictions and LIME explanations into natural-language diagnostic guidance for farmers, and conducting a more extensive, systematic exploratory data analysis to guide future data collection toward the classes and conditions the model currently finds hardest.
