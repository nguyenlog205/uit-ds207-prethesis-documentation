\section{Dataset Construction}
\subsection{Data Collection and Filtering}
\label{subsec:data_collection}

All images were collected by our team in avocado orchards across three provinces of the Central Highlands: Gia Lai, Đắk Lắk, and Đắk Nông. We visited multiple gardens to capture the natural diversity of leaf appearance across (1) light (full sun, cloudy, backlight), (2) weather (dry, cloudy, sunny, after rain), (3) time of day (morning, noon, afternoon), and (4) angle and distance (top-down, oblique, close-up).

To ensure quality, we applied the following filtering steps:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item[(1)] Removal of duplicate images;
    \item[(2)] Exclusion of images with motion blur so severe that the leaf becomes impossible to distinguish;
    \item[(3)] Manual screening to discard frames where the avocado leaf is occluded more than $50\%$ or is otherwise impossible to identify reliably.
\end{enumerate}
After filtering, $1,861$ images remained and were passed to the annotation phase.


\subsection{Annotation Guideline and Metadata Definition}
\label{subsec:annotation_guideline}

We defined a simple annotation protocol for the six avocado leaf conditions observed in our dataset. The six class labels and their meanings are:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \texttt{binhthuong} – healthy leaf, no visible damage or disease.
    \item \texttt{chaymepla} – Leaf scorch (no causal pathogen; abiotic physiological disorder): tip or margin burn typically caused by salt stress or sunburn.
    \item \texttt{domla} – Cercospora leaf spot (\textit{Pseudocercospora purpurea}): small brown to purplish-black lesions caused by fungal infection.
    \item \texttt{gisat} – Algal leaf spot, locally known as ``red rust'' (\textit{Cephaleuros virescens}): orange, velvety pustule-like spots caused by a parasitic alga.
    \item \texttt{moctrang} – Powdery mildew (\textit{Oidium} sp., family Erysiphaceae): white powdery coating on the leaf surface.
    \item \texttt{repsap} – Long-tailed mealybug damage (\textit{Pseudococcus longispinus}): sap-feeding insect leaving silver or stippled marks.
\end{itemize}

The annotators followed a consistent guideline:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Each image must contain at least one clearly visible avocado leaf as the main subject.
    \item The label is assigned based on the dominant visible symptom on that leaf. If multiple symptoms are present, the annotator selects the most severe condition.
    \item Images where the leaf cannot be reliably identified (e.g., extreme blur, heavy occlusion) are discarded during manual screening.
\end{itemize}

All annotations were recorded in a simple CSV file with \texttt{filename} and \texttt{label} columns. No bounding boxes or additional metadata were required, as the task is image-level classification.


\subsection{Quality Control}
\label{subsec:quality_control}

To ensure the reliability of the annotated dataset, we conducted a quality control step using an independent expert review.

\paragraph{Annotation aggregation.}
After three annotators independently labelled the entire dataset, the final label for each image was determined by majority voting. In the rare case of a three‑way tie (all three annotators disagree), the image was flagged for a short discussion to reach a unanimous decision; no such case occurred in practice.

\paragraph{Expert review procedure.}
From the $1,861$ images with their consensus labels, we randomly sampled $15\%$ ($279$ images), independent of the train/validation/test split used for model development. These images were presented to an avocado leaf disease expert who had not participated in the original annotation. The expert, blind to the annotators' decision, assigned one of the six predefined classes to each image following the same annotation guideline. As only one expert was involved, the resulting agreement reflects consistency with a single reviewer's judgment rather than inter-expert reliability.

\paragraph{Agreement metric.}
We measured the agreement between the annotator consensus labels and the expert labels using Cohen's Kappa coefficient ($\kappa$), which accounts for chance agreement. It is defined as:
\begin{equation}
\kappa = \frac{p_o - p_e}{1 - p_e}
\end{equation}
where $p_o$ is the observed proportion of agreement and $p_e$ the expected proportion under random assignment. Values of $\kappa > 0.80$ indicate strong agreement \cite{landis1977measurement}.
